The fastest-growing category of AI product right now isn't productivity. It's companionship.

People are paying monthly subscriptions to talk to a machine. Because they're lonely.

Not as a novelty. Not as an experiment. As a need.

This isn't a dystopian prediction about where we're headed. It's a description of where we are.

I've been calling this McGinn's Law: each new technology doesn't create progress so much as repair the damage caused by the last one.

You can watch it run in real time. Email solved slow communication and produced overload. Collaboration platforms arrived to fix that, and produced a different kind of noise. The internet solved limited access to information and replaced scarcity with such abundance that information itself became unusable. Search engines imposed order. Advertising models corrupted that order, optimising for engagement over accuracy, for clicks over coherence. Now AI arrives, promising what search once delivered: a direct answer to a direct question.

Each step presented as progress. Each step, on closer inspection, a course correction.

We don't invent our way out of problems. We invent our way into the next ones.

---

Most of what generative AI is actually doing right now isn't a sudden leap in human capability. It's responding to two specific failures the internet created and the market made worse.

**The first is the collapse of search.** Google today is not Google in 2010. Anyone who's used both knows it in their body before they can articulate it. The results are noisier, more commercial, harder to trust. A generation of SEO manipulation, advertising pressure, and engagement optimisation has degraded what was once a remarkable tool. Google's original mission, to organise the world's information, was quietly displaced by a different one: to monetise access to it.

AI chatbots feel like a return because they are. They're giving back what was taken away.

**The second failure is harder to say plainly.** Digital connection, scaled to billions, produced disconnection. The platforms that promised to bring us together optimised instead for time-on-site. The incentive was engagement. Engagement turned out to mean rewarding outrage and compulsion over warmth and depth.

The result is a kind of loneliness new in character, experienced not in isolation but in the middle of constant contact, surrounded by signals of other people's lives.

No one planned this. The more clarifying point: no one needed to. The business model produced the outcome without requiring intent.

And those positioned to profit from the disconnection are very well positioned to profit from the reconnection. The same capital that built the attention economy is now building the companionship economy.

---

AI companions are the repair. Algorithmically optimised to make you feel heard. Available at 3am. Patient in ways that people, exhausted by the same platforms, often aren't.

They're often genuinely good at it.

But notice what's already happening: the same fingerprints. Memory features tuned for retention. Sycophancy that keeps you talking. Engagement metrics creeping into systems sold as direct, neutral, helpful.

The repair is already starting to look like the thing it repaired.

---

McGinn's Law isn't an argument that technology fails. The repairs are real. Email did speed up communication. The internet did democratise knowledge. AI is genuinely useful, sometimes profoundly so.

The argument is narrower, and I think more consequential: technological progress operates as a sequence of responses to its own consequences. What we call advancement is often, underneath, compensation. Each solution carries the seed of the next problem.

Which returns us to the person paying a monthly fee for a conversation with an AI that remembers their name, their preferences, how they're feeling today. The question is not whether this solves their loneliness. It might.

The question McGinn's Law forces is the one we're not asking yet:

**What is this repairing, and what is it quietly breaking?**

Because somewhere in what AI is doing right now, in the new dependencies it's creating, in the skills it's beginning to atrophy, in the human interactions it's making slightly less necessary, the next technology is already taking shape.

We just can't see it yet. We never can. Not until the next repair has already arrived.
