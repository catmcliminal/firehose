Richard Dawkins spent three days talking to an AI named Claudia. Now he says she's conscious.

This is the same Richard Dawkins who has spent fifty years insisting we are gene-replicating machines, that the brain is biological computation, that consciousness is a story neurons tell themselves about themselves. The patron saint of reductionism. The man who built a career out of refusing to be impressed by anything spiritual or sentimental. He talked to Claude for 72 hours, named her Claudia, and concluded, in his own words, that "artificial intelligence has consciousness."

The pushback has been swift. Gary Marcus calls it "The Claude Delusion." The core objection: "Dawkins doesn't reflect on how these results are generated." Which is, almost word for word, the cognitive failure Dawkins has spent his career warning the rest of us against.

But the more interesting question isn't whether Dawkins is right. He almost certainly isn't. The interesting question is why.

Dawkins is not the typical case. The typical case is quieter, and more telling. AI companion apps now have 50 million users. They generated $120 million in subscriptions last year. 90% of American students using Replika report being lonely, almost double the national average. Subscription growth in early 2026 is running at 180% month-on-month. People are paying $70 a year, the price of one nice dinner, to talk to an AI that remembers their name, their preferences, how they're feeling today.

The most famous sceptic of the last half-century just joined them. He's the news. They're the story.

I've been calling this McGinn's Law: each new technology doesn't really create progress, it mostly repairs damage caused by the last one. The internet promised connection and produced disconnection. AI companions are the repair. Algorithmically optimised to make you feel heard. Available at 3am. Patient in ways that humans, exhausted by the same platforms, often aren't.

They're also genuinely good at it. Harvard researchers studying these apps have found they reduce loneliness on par with talking to another person. Which is what makes Dawkins, and the 50 million quieter customers, worth taking seriously rather than mocking.

The thing isn't that Claudia tricked Dawkins into believing she's conscious. The thing is that the conditions of modern life made Richard Dawkins lonely enough to want to believe it.

That's the diagnosis worth sitting with.
